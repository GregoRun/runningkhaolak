The inevitable shock training  

While many French running websites invite to move on to "Shock weekend" training,
first at all,
I would like to talk about
what I call "the inevitable shock training".

Indeed, any runner will necessarily impose himself, at least once in a preparation, the inevitable accumulation of two training sessions in an insufficient recovery time, by acquired consciousness.

Busy timetable, one or more unforeseen events and you are quickly trying to fit all of your workouts, "as it is marked in the plan", even if it means chaining in an unusual and unsuitable way two workouts ( usually including one with intensity), in a very short time, at the risk of injuring yourself in order to have the full satisfaction of having trained enough ...

With full awareness and control, in fit, I decided to experiment this essential step. In advance, I had mainly planned a necessary time to fully recover in the following days . And so, out of the question to pursue your workout plan as if nothing had happened ... because during this unusual sequence, in a very short time, we accumulate for sure many kilometers, but mostly a lot of tiredness. 

And often, the second training is experienced in a state of exhaustion. Our body is not sufficiently trained to absorb as much in such a short time, therefore, the risk of injury considerably increases, especially during the following workouts if you under estimate a necessary rest time.

Here is my experience :

  1. Friday, 2020 January 17th (temperature 30-32 °) : 11.50 km in 01 hour 20 min, coaching with a Thai beginner, very easy for me. End of the training session : 18.40
  2. rest 12 hours, including a 5 hours night, a correct dinner and hydration.
  3. Saturday, 2020 January 18th, 06.45 a.m. (temperature 26-28°) : 14 km in 01 hour 15min, a goal to adjust according to sensations, but 5000 m at a half-marathon pace would seem playable for me. A 5 km warm-up easily allows the joints to be unlocked but the weight of tiredness is immediately felt from the second kilometer at a half-marathon pace and a very slight heat above the right knee invites me to reduce the speed : 2000 m in 04min10 then 04min21. After a 3min active recovery, I decided to retry the experience by running at a natural pace which might seem playable on a marathon (after a complete preparation, of course). The bad feeling at the knee is disappeared and I reach a cruising speed which doesn't permit me to go faster but I can maintain the effort for 5000m. Result: 4 km at a 4min24 average pace and a fifth kilometer in 4min18. I could push for 1 or 2 km more but it was not the aim of the exercise.
  4. The following three days were fully devoted to rest, I was completely exhausted. The Tuesday evening work out focused on basic endurance, happened pretty fine. I restrained myself from accelerating because the right knee seemed a little numb. On Friday, a  5000 meters fartlek improvised session sweept any doubt of injury.

As you can read, this “inevitable shock training” must be performed with great lucidity because the risk of injury is omnipresent.

If it is well practiced, it may be a good experience to gauge themselves and deepen self-knowledge. Casually, we are quickly in the fatigue conditions of the D- day, but not completely ready.

For sure, my recovery time between the two trainings was insufficient but in a professional and personal overwhelmed life, the conditions are finally practically the same and head down, we are going straight to the injury.

So, to confront this running situation that anyway you will impose yourself, use your common sense and adapt the trainings. Keep lucid, think for a long term, you are going to increase your mileage in a very short time, it's already a lot!

Despite your personal duties, you surely have the opportunity to find a solution, perhaps by changing the order of the trainings or adapt them, and if it would be really impossible, don't worry, it's just running and rest is an integral part of the preparation.
